Unit 4. Part 2

Nouns (Grammar)

Cases

To fulfill various syntactic functions in a sentence, nouns and pronouns in some languages have an extensive declension system.

In other languages, these functions are performed by prepositions and postpositions.

English and Armenian have both, although Armenian has a more elaborate system of noun and pronoun declensions.

English nouns change forms either to express possession or when they are used in the plural (cf. father’s, fathers).

Nouns in Armenian have an elaborate declension paradigm, which may overwhelm the English speaker.

They change forms either by taking case markers, by undergoing vowel alternations or inner permutations, or by doing both.

Armenian has seven cases

  • nominative,
  • accusative,
  • genitive,
  • dative,
  • ablative,
  • instrumental,
  • locative.

The first 4 cases form two pairs that may correspond in form but differ in syntactic function

  • nominative and accusative,
  • genitive and dative,

Nominative

The nominative case in singular is the citation form of nouns as they appear in dictionaries.

It is the initial form in a declension paradigm and has no explicit case markers.

It can, however, be used with or without determiners, i.e. a definite article -ը/-ն or a possessive article ending in -ս, -դ, -ը/-ն.

Noun in the nominative case may be

  • subject,
  • subject complement.

The noun in the nominative case serves primarily as the subject of a sentence

Անահիտը վառ գույներ էր սիրում։

anahitə var gujneɾ‿eɾ siɾum

Anahit liked bright colors.

Գորգս նոր է։

goɾgᵊs noɾ‿e

My carpet is new.

In the above sentences, Անահիտը 'Anahit' and գորգս'my carpet' are subjects and appear in the nominative case.

They can be elicited in response to the questions respectively

ո՞վov?who?

Անահիտը

ի՞նչըinʧʰə?what?

գորգը

As subject complement in compound nominal predicate. The secondary syntactic function of the nominative case is also to serve as subject complement, linked to the subject by the auxiliary verb եմ or other copular verbs

Եղբայրս բժիշկ է։

jeʁpajɾᵊs bᵊʒiʃk‿e

My brother is a physician.

Արամը մնում է ընկերս։

aɾamə mᵊnum‿e ənkeɾᵊs

Aram remains my friend.

In the first sentence, both the subject եղբայրս'my brother' and its complement բժիշկ'physician' appear in the nominative case.

They are linked to each other by the copula է'is'. In the second sentence, it is the verb մնալ'to remain' that links the subject Արամը 'Aram' to ընկերս'my friend' and both nouns are used in the nominative case.

Functions of Accusative

In a sentence, the accusative performs the following functions

  • direct object,
  • local adverb,
  • temporal adverbial,
  • adverbial indicating measurements and sizes.

A direct object governed by a transitive verb

Հայրը գովում է որդուն։ (ո՞ւմ)

hajɾə govum‿e ʋoɾtʰun

The father praises his son.

Նկարներս տեսա՞ր։ (ի՞նչ)

nᵊkaɾneɾᵊs tesaɾ?

Have you seen my pictures?

In these sentences, որդուն'the son' and նկարներս'my pictures' are direct objects of the transitive verbs գովել'to praise', տեսնել'to see' respectively.

Note that in the first sentence, the question asked is ո՞ւմ'whom?' with reference to որդուն'the son' and that both the question and the form of the noun որդի'son' correspond to the dative case.

In the second sentence the question ի՞նչ'what?' refers to an non-person; hence the form նկարներս'my pictures' that coincides with the nominative case.

A local adverb that answers the question ո՞ւրwhere?

Վերջապես տուն եկա։

veɾʧʰapes tun jeka

At last I came home.

Երբեմն գրադարան էի գնում։

jeɾpʰemᵊn gɾadaɾan‿eji gᵊnum

I used to go to the library sometimes.

Here, the unmarked nouns տուն'home' and գրադարան'library' are adverbials of place and non-person.

A temporal adverbal that answers the question ե՞րբwhen?

Մի օր էլ նա անհետացավ։

mi oɾ el na anhetaʦʰav

One day he disappeared.

Հաջորդ տարի կտեսնվենք։

haʤoɾtʰ taɾi kᵊtesnᵊvenkʰ

Next year we will see each other.

Here, օր'day' and տարի'year' are unmarked nouns used as adverbials of time and non-person.

An adverbial indicating measurements and sizes (miles, kilograms, pounds, liters, etc.) and answers the question որքա՞նhow?

Երեք բաժակ գինի խմեցի։

jeɾekʰ baʒak gini χᵊmeʦʰi

I drank three glasses of wine.

Որքա՞ն գինի խմեցիր։

voɾkʰan? gini χᵊmeʦʰiɾ

How much wine did you drink?

Տասը մղոն քայլեցինք։

tasə mᵊʁon kʰajleʦʰinkʰ

We walked ten miles.

Որքա՞ն քայլեցիք։

voɾkʰan? kʰajleʦʰikʰ

How long did you walk?

In the examples above, երեք բաժակ'three glasses' and տասը մղոն'ten miles' take the accusative case as non-person.

Plural forms

The nominative plural takes the ending -ներ, -եր which can appear with or without determiners, i.e. a definite article -ը/-ն or possessive article -ս, -դ, -ը/-ն

Երեխաներն ուշացան։

jeɾeχaneɾn uʃaʦʰan

The children are late.

This does not apply to nouns that have the plural ending -ք, -իկ, -այք as these plural endings do not take the articles -ը / -ն

Մարդիկ ուշացան։

maɾtʰikʰ uʃaʦʰan

The people are late.

Տիկնայք ուշացան։

tiknajkʰ uʃaʦʰan

The the ladies are late.

For the direct object, the accusative plural makes the same person vs. non-person distinction as the accusative singular. Nouns indicating persons use their genitive-dative case while nouns for non-persons use the nominative-accusative form

Պաշտպանե՜նք երեխաներին։ (ո՞ւմ)

paʃtpanenkʰ! jeɾeχaneɾin

Let’s protect the children!

Պաշտպանե՜նք մեր անտառները։ (ի՞նչ)

paʃtpanenkʰ! meɾ antarneɾə

Let’s protect our forests!

Armenian-English Contrasts

Like all languages, Armenian has conventionally established conversation units, or gambits, which are bound by socio-cultural conventions and, most of the time, defy literal translation into other languages.

Some expressions of politeness

  • խնդրեմχᵊntʰɾemplease,
  • կարելի՞ էkaɾeli‿e?may I?,
  • համեցե՛քhameʦʰekʰ!come! please! kindly!,
  • անուշanuʃsweet.

խնդրեմ

There is not always a one-to-one correspondence between the Armenian խնդրեմ and the English 'please'. There are cases where, when Armenian uses խնդրեմ, English has other conventional expressions.

a) Frequently used in polite requests and replies. As such, it corresponds to the English 'please'

Ասա՛ խնդրեմ…

asa! χᵊntʰɾem…

Tell me, please… (inf.)

Ասացե՛ք խնդրեմ…

asaʦʰekʰ! χᵊntʰɾem…

Tell me, please… (form.)

– Պատուհանը փակե՞մ։

patuhanə pʰakem?

Shall I close the window?

– Այո՛, խնդրեմ։

ajo!, χᵊntʰɾem

Yes, please.

b) Used to make commands sound milder

Դուռը բա՛ց արեք, խնդրեմ։

durə baʦʰ! aɾekʰ, χᵊntʰɾem!

Open the door, please!

c) Serves as a prompt response to 'thank you!'

– Շնորհակա՜լ եմ։

ʃᵊnoɾakal‿em!

Thank you! You are welcome!

– Խնդրե՜մ։

χᵊntʰɾem!

Don’t mention it! My pleasure!

d) Employed alone when offering an item to someone

– Խնդրե՜մ։

χᵊntʰɾem!

Here you are!

e) Serves as a reply to a request for permission

– Թույլ կտա՞ք ձեր թերթը կարդալ։

tʰujl kᵊtakʰ? ʣeɾ tʰeɾtʰə kaɾtʰal

May I please read your newspaper?

– Խնդրե՜մ։

χᵊntʰɾem!

By all means! Sure! Please do!

կարելի

The very common conversation tool կարելի՞ էkaɾeli e?'may I?' is used to ask for permission.

Before entering a room

– Կարելի՞ է։

kaɾeli‿e?

May I (come in)?

Before doing something

– Կարելի՞ է փորձել։

kaɾeli‿e poɾʦʰel?

May I try it?

Before saying something

– Կարելի՞ է մի բան ասել։

kaɾeli‿e? mi ban asel

May I say something?

համեցեք

Means 'come! please! kindly!'

As a polite response to a request for permission

– Կարելի՞ է։

May I?

– Համեցե՛ք։

Please do!

As a polite incitement to accept an offer of food or drinks

– Համեցե՛ք։

Help yourself!

As a welcome to guests entering the house

– Համեցե՛ք։

Come in, please!

անուշ

In everyday talk, there are a few expressions of kindness and hospitality, containing the word անուշanuʃsweet, enjoy.

– Համեցե՜ք։

hameʦek!

Help yourself!

– Անո՜ւշ արա։

anuʃ! aɾa (inf.)

Enjoy it!

– Ճաշը շատ համեղ է։

ʧaʃə ʃat hameʁ e

The meal is very tasty.

Անո՜ւշ լինի։

anuʃ! lini

May it be sweet!

Կենա՜ցդ։

kenaʦʰᵊt!

To your health! (informal)

Ձե՜ր կենացը։

ʣeɾ! kenaʦʰə

To your health! (formal/pl.)

– Անո՜ւշ լինի։

anuʃ! lini

I am glad you enjoyed it!

Pronunciation

դ, տ, թ

Rather than a double system of stop consonants, i.e. the voiced d and the voiceless t, East Armenian has a triple system. The triple system of the related consonants դ, տ, թ can be presented as follows

  • Դ դdvoiced,
  • Տ տtvoiceless non-aspirated,
  • Թ թtʰvoiceless aspirated.

Like in the case of բ, պ, թ each of these consonants is an independent phoneme, i.e. the smallest phonetic unit that distinguishes one word from another. The following three words differ in meaning in the contrast of the phonemic consonants դ, տ, թ
